Palsabangon Mangrove Swamp Forest Reserve , covering 587 hectares across Brgy. Palsabangon and Brgy. Pinagbayanan, is the largest mangrove ecosystem in Quezon Province. Once threatened by illegal activities, it now thrives under NIPAS protection. Home to diverse species like crabs, birds, clams, and monitor lizards, it features a hanging bridge, five-story view deck, and kayak-friendly mangrove tunnels. Now a key ecotourism and research site, it draws students, vloggers, and even Miss Earth delegates.

Palsabangon Mangrove Swamp



The Palsabangon Mangrove Swamp Forest Reserve , located in Brgy. Palsabangon and part of Brgy. Pinagbayanan in Pagbilao, Quezon, spans 587 hectares and stretches 9.97 km. Accessible by land or boat, it features mangrove forests, fishponds, rice fields, and coconut plantations. Inside the reserve are key facilities like a PASu Office, event hall, nursery, and a five-story view deck. Visitors enter through a hanging bridge surrounded by mangrove species like bungalon, bakauan lalaki, and bakauan babae. The soil varies from sandy loam to clay, and five rivers and creeks flow through it. The reserve shelters diverse wildlifeâ€”fish, crabs, clams, monitor lizards, snakes, and birdsâ€”including resident, endemic, and migrant species categorized by rarity and feeding behavior such as piscivores, herbivores, and insectivores.

Binahaan River Watershed Forest Reserve



The Binahaan Watershed Forest Reserve , located in Sitio Logpond, Barangay Binahaan, Pagbilao, Quezon, lies 150 kilometers from Manila and 2 to 5 kilometers off the Maharlika Highway. Covering varied terrainâ€”from plains and valleys to steep gullies and rugged mountainsâ€”the area is dominated by Bolinao clay and sandy clay loam soils. The watershed hosts old-growth and secondary dipterocarp forests interspersed with degraded grasslands and ravines. It supports rich biodiversity, including rufous hornbills, bleeding-heart pigeons, Philippine deer, monkeys, and wild pigs, alongside abundant insect and plant life such as Dipterocarpus, Shorea, Hopea, and rattan species. Its scenic mountain views, natural waterfalls, and wildlife presence make it valuable for both conservation and eco-tourism.

The Quezon Protected Landscape in Pagbilao, Quezon, is a 566.10-hectare portion of a larger forest reserve shared with Atimonan and Padre Burgos. Declared a national park in 1934 and now protected under the NIPAS and ENIPAS Acts, it features lush forests, waterfalls, nature trails, and diverse wildlife including hornbills and Philippine deer. Sites like Amao Falls, Buenavista Spot, and the iconic EME Zigzag Road attract tourists, hikers, and researchers alike.

Quezon Protected Landscape (QPL)



The Pagbilao part of the Quezon Protected Landscape encompasses 566.10 hectares within the larger 938-hectare protected area situated along the southern Sierra Madre Mountain Range in Quezon Province. This section includes natural attractions such as Amao Falls, EME Road, Buenavista Spot, the View Deck, and the PASu Office and Picnic Area. Originally established as Quezon National Park in 1934 and later expanded and reclassified under the NIPAS Act of 1992, the area is now protected under Republic Act No. 11038 or the ENIPAS Act of 2018. It is one of the few remaining forested landscapes in the CALABARZON region and plays a vital ecological role by housing dipterocarp-dominated tropical rainforests, rare wildlife species, mineral deposits, and freshwater sources. The terrain of the protected landscape ranges from flat lands to steep mountain slopes, covered in a dense forest canopy that supports a rich array of flora and fauna. Trees such as Dipterocarpus, Shorea, Parashorea, and Pentacme are common, along with non-dipterocarp species like Ficus, Syzygium, and rattan. Wildlife such as Philippine macaques, deer, rufous hornbills, and endangered birds like the spotted wood kingfisher and Luzon little crow thrive in this habitat. Sites like the Amao Falls feature an 8-stage cascade where trekkers can earn a certificate for completing the trail. Meanwhile, the View Deck in Silangang Malicboy offers breathtaking vistas of Tayabas Bay, dense forest canopies, and wildlife in their natural environment. The area is not only a natural haven but also a center for education and community development. With strict guidelines in place, tourists must be accompanied by trained guides, and efforts are ongoing to improve eco-tourism facilities like the picnic areas and natural pools. Historically, the region played a role during the Japanese era, and local lore even speaks of hidden treasures within the forest. The site continues to support biodiversity conservation while serving as a recreational, scientific, and cultural destination for locals and visitors alike.
