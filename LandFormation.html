More about Patayan Island



Patayan Island , a 26.98-hectare landmass off the coast of Brgy. Pinagbayanan in Pagbilao, Quezon, is a unique island known for its inverted-liver shape when viewed from the map. Surrounded by the barangays of Pinagbayanan, Bantigue, Mapagong, and Palsabangon, the island lies 20 to 30 minutes away by boat from Daungan Port, depending on sea conditions and boat speed. It is famed for its "tulay buhangin" or vanishing sandbar, which appears only at dawn and fades as the tide rises. The shoreline is mostly covered with white pebbles and pyrite nodules, giving it a white-sand effect from afar. Rich in biodiversity, Patayan Island is surrounded by mangrove vegetation such as bakawan, pipisik, aroma, and pagatpat, which serve as habitat for fish like talakitok, baliwis, and kanuping, as well as birds, snakes, and monitor lizards. The island also offers stunning views of the sunrise, sunset, and the Team Energy power plant across the water. Historically, the island is shrouded in mystery and folklore. Its name, "Patayan," is believed to have originated from stories passed down through generations, suggesting that the island was once a site of pirate executions. Locals like Mr. Pedro Macarulay and Brgy. Captain Rennan Salles support this theory, though no physical evidence remains. Ownership of the island has changed hands over the years, beginning with Mr. Borlongan, followed by the Sio family, who lived on the island during World War II, and later by the Santayana family. Although there was an attempt to rename it "Calilayan Island," the original name was eventually restored. Today, only three families reside on the island, serving as caretakers of both the fishpond owned by politician Proceso Alcala and the private properties scattered throughout the area. While Patayan Island is a favorite summer destination for tourists who come to enjoy its serene landscape and picturesque views, it also faces environmental and jurisdictional threats, including erosion, unregulated tourism, and climate change. Despite these challenges, the island remains a symbol of Pagbilao's natural beauty, cultural depth, and enduring folklore.

Balugbog Baboy Sandbar



Balugbog Baboy Sandbar , located between Brgy. Bantigue and Brgy. Dalahican in Pagbilao, Quezon, is a stunning natural formation named for its resemblance to a pig's spine ("balugbog" meaning spine and "baboy" meaning pig). Spanning over 250,000 square meters, the sandbar is known for its golden sands, crab holes, and higher elevation, making it the first to appear during low tide compared to nearby sandbars. Locals and tourists often visit for its serene beauty, easy access, and scenic views, especially during sunrise and sunset. While scientific explanations describe its growth as natural sediment buildup, some residents still believe the sandbar is protected by unseen guardians. Fishermen even rely on it as a refuge during storms. Over the years, it has also become a favorite destination of celebrities like Zsa Zsa Padilla and Karylle, who are drawn to its peaceful and unique charm.

Bilaran Sandbar



Bilaran Sandbar , located 9 kilometers off the coast of Pagbilao, Quezon, is a crescent-shaped stretch of white sand known for its scenic backdrop of Mount Banahaw, Pagbilao Bay, and the Team Energy power plant. The sandbar measures around 546 meters at its tip and about 152 meters at its widest point, though its size varies with the tide. The clear waters surrounding it are home to a diverse marine ecosystem, including sea urchins, starfish, jellyfish, clams, snails, and crabs. Local lore attributes the sandbar's crescent shape to the strong currents from Tayabas Bay pushing sand inward. Historically, Bilaran was used by fishermen to dry their catch and take shelter during storms. According to SAMBACA President Isidro Sartin, the sandbar once stood about 15 feet highâ€”its peak even had a nipa hut for resting. Over the years, quarrying flattened its elevation, and stories of mysterious lights known as "Santelmo" add an eerie charm to the place. In 2001, a ship bound for Masbate caught fire nearby, and Team Energy employees helped rescue passengers. Between 2017 and 2018, a floating cottage built by SAMBACA members brought renewed interest to the sandbar, even catching the attention of GMA's "Kapuso Mo, Jessica Soho." The site's fame grew as celebrities like Coco Martin, Yeng Constantino, and Zsa Zsa Padilla visited, with Padilla featuring it in her vlog. During extreme low tides, Bilaran appears to connect with two nearby sandbars, forming a formation locals call "Balugbog Baboy" due to its resemblance to a pig's spine. Registered groups like SAMBACA now offer island-hopping tours that include Bilaran, Kwebang Lampas, and Isla Salamin, which support the community through tourism income and mussel harvesting. Despite facing threats such as over-tourism, sand mining, and misinformation, Bilaran Sandbar remains a treasured ecological and cultural landmark in Pagbilao.

Daungan Pari



Daungan Pari , located in Sitio Daungan Pari, Brgy. Binahaan, Pagbilao, Quezon, lies about 9.4 kilometers off the main highway and connects directly to the Binahaan River and Pagbilao's seawaters. The area is characterized by its muddy shoreline, common among mangrove zones and estuaries, and is home to a range of marine life and waterbirds. Upon arrival, one notices the presence of informal settlers, colorful fishing boats, and a mix of natural greenery. A concrete chapel now stands near the water's edge, accompanied by an 8-foot cross, marking its religious importance. According to local history and a plaque mounted on the chapel wall, this spot is where Franciscan missionaries first arrived in 1688, making it the birthplace of Christianity in Pagbilao. The original chapel, made of bamboo and cogon, stood here before the population moved inland to establish the current town center. The present-day concrete chapel was built between 2017 and 2019 with the help of the 609 Gun Club, Father Joseph Estrema, and local leaders. Folklore adds mystique to the site. Local accounts speak of hidden treasuresâ€”gold bars and a bell said to be buried along with a Franciscan priest. A rock engraved with "CARDO" and "RIP" supposedly marked the burial site, but it was later stolen. Residents also recount eerie sightings: a headless priest and a vanishing black pig, contributing to the site's rich tapestry of history, faith, and legend.

Balete Beach



Balete Beach , tucked in a remote corner of Pagbilao Grande Island behind the Pagbilao Power Station, is one of the town's most underrated yet naturally captivating destinations. It features a quiet stretch of powdery white sand, crystal-clear waters, and views of Patayan Island and the sunset. Accessible by a 20-minute boat ride from Pantalan ng Pagbilao or a 1-kilometer walk from Jenny's Beach, the path to Balete Beach is lined with balete and aroma trees. The beach is known for its colorful pebbles, coral rocks, and lush coastal greenery, including ancient mango and yucca plants. Its rocky areas require caution, making aqua shoes advisable. A few remnants of its pastâ€”such as a comfort room and stairsâ€”reveal its history as a resort managed by the Garcia clan in the 1980s. According to longtime resident Virgilio Calizo Sr., Balete Beach was once owned by the Glodoviza-Glorioso clan and later sold to the Garcias, who transformed it into a private resort with bamboo and cogon cottages. It was even used for drying coconuts and as a quarry site, where locals believed Spanish treasures were hidden. Calizo recalled finding gold coins along streams after bad weather, which locals once used for bartering. Balete Beach has its share of legendsâ€”stories of a giant face in the cottage window, a crying girl, and a glowing fireball known as santelmo believed to mark hidden treasure sites. Film buffs may also recognize it as the shooting location of the 1975 film Madugong Daigdig ni Salvacion, starring Ricky Belmonte and Cristina Ponce. Locals like Martin Calizo and the Pornela family also reported eerie sightings of half-bodied creatures and mysterious beings by the trees, which some still believe protect the beach and its secrets.

Lima-aw Mountain



Lima-aw Mountain is located at the heart of Pagbilao Grande Island and is characterized by its closely grouped peaks and ridges resembling a cluster of similarly built homes. With steep slopes and sharp or rounded edges, most of its summits are pointed, except for one distinctively flat peak at the center, which offers a natural vantage point. Visible even from the main road, some of its caves and rock formations hint at the mountain's raw, rugged beauty. At the mountain's base, signs of erosion and fallen rocks suggest natural wear over time. Stories tied to Lima-aw Mountain reveal its mystical and historical depth. Jose Estrope, a longtime resident and caretaker at the foot of the mountain, claims to have met a spirit named "Carding" who gifted him a magical five-peso coin that returned to his pocket after each use. Bound by a vow of secrecy, Jose fell ill when he revealed the coin's secret and was later healed by a faith healer who tattooed a protective charm on his chest. His wife, Asuncion, shared how their family eventually moved away from the mountain for safety. Their granddaughter, Christine, now tends to their buffaloes near Lima-aw and observes native wildlife like monkeys and hawks. Meanwhile, Jonathan Edrad recounted his grandfather's wartime memory of seeing a Japanese autogyro land on Lima-aw's flat peak, adding a historical layer to the mountain's enigmatic reputation.

Kwebang Lampas



Kwebang Lampas , located in Brgy. Ibabang Polo, Pagbilao, Quezon, is a natural sea cave known for its twin openings, turquoise waters, and dramatic rock formations. Just a 15-minute boat ride from Sitio Tubahin, this cave beside Lukang Beach is best visited during low tide, when swimmers can pass through its tunnel-like structure. Inside, the cave is lined with algae, stalagmites, and stalactites in shades of gray and yellow, while the water underneath mixes warm and cold currents. Local legends and eerie stories surround the cave, including sightings of an "engkanto" in the form of a white monkey and a bulldozer mysteriously operating on its own. Over the years, Kwebang Lampas has gained fame not only for its natural beauty but also as a filming location for classic Filipino movies such as Perlas ng Silangan (1969), Pedro Penduko (1974), and Naked Paradise (1985). Today, it continues to attract tourists, adventurers, and photographers eager to experience its blend of mystery and charm.

Mangilalan Cave



Mangilalan Cave , nestled deep in the forests of Brgy. Ilayang Bagumbungan, is one of the most recognized caves in Quezon. Covered in moss and local plants like "lukmoy" (Epipremnum pinnatum), the cave features a narrow entrance and a cool interior with a natural waterfall. Inside, a unique "Lamesang Bato" or stone table with a chalice-like dip is treated like a holy font. In 1983, local resident Apolinario Diala discovered a Japanese soldier's helmet inside, believed to be from wartime, though it mysteriously vanished soon after. Locals also tell of a Japanese man who once lived by the cave with a Filipina wife. Despite the darkness and bats, Father Obena of St. Catherine Parish once held a full Catholic mass inside, adding to the cave's rich spiritual and historical legacy.
