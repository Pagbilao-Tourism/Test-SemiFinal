Despite its narrow and often slippery path, especially during rains, the road remains well-patrolled by volunteer aides and offers a lush, forested environment with cool mountain air. Known locally as Bitukang Manok for its intestine-like shape, it presents both a challenging and scenic journey for drivers. Historically, the EME Road was discovered by Alfredo Castro around 1904â€“1905 and constructed in 1929 under the direction of American engineer Julius Vogelgesang during President Manuel L. Quezon's administration. It was completed in 1935 after six years and later improved in 1969 during the Marcos era. Initially perilous due to unprotected cliffs, the road has since been reinforced with safety features. It played a significant role during World War II, serving as a supply route for Japanese forces following their landing at Lamon Bay in Atimonan. In recent years, natural disasters such as Typhoon Mario in 2014 and heavy rains in 2018 caused damage and prompted restoration efforts. Today, the EME Road is not only an essential transportation route but also a tourist attraction and cultural icon, beloved for its natural beauty and steeped in both history and myth.

Located in Barangay Silangang Malicboy, Pagbilao, the old Philippine National Railway Station is one of the earliest in Quezon, with an estimated floor area of 80 square meters. The foundation is made of concrete, while the upper structure was originally built with hardwood, including wooden windows and a now-deteriorated roof. Red and green paint remains faintly visible near the ground. The station is surrounded by trees, nearby homes, and the railway line on its right side, still marked by scattered support rocks. Opened in 1914, the Malicboy Station was part of the Manilaâ€“Legazpi line, approximately 156 km from Tutuban. It once served passenger and cargo trains such as the Lucena Express, Penafrancia Express, and Mayon Limited (1988), all now defunct. With the rise of road transport, railway operations declined, and no repairs or restorations have since been made to the station, leaving it in its current state of quiet decay.

Pagbilao Railroad Station



The Pagbilao Railroad Station , located just 40 meters from the town's public market, was once a vital hub of transport and trade in Quezon Province. Constructed in 1914 during the Manila Railroad Company era (later renamed Philippine National Railways in 1960), the station featured narra wood trusses and walls, a Dutch-style double door, a tool storage room, and screened metal windows. Surrounded by banana trees and remnants of construction materials, it stood as a modest yet functional structure. For 92 years, it served routes connecting Manila to Lucena, Pagbilao to Naga, and onward to Legazpi, providing local residents with access to transportation, commerce, and opportunity. However, its operations ceased in 2006 after Typhoon Milenyo severely damaged the station and destroyed the nearby Mamala Bridge, cutting off access and halting rail service. Though now abandoned, the station remains a nostalgic landmark in Pagbilao's history.

The Gudani ancestral house in Brgy. Sta. Catalina, Pagbilao, sits on a 300-square-meter lot near the public market and tricycle terminal.
